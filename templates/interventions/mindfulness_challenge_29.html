Graded Tasks for Physical Activity

Let's talk about graded tasks for the promotion of physical activity. Graded tasks begin as easy-to-perform tasks, and become increasingly difficult. For example, you could walk for 100 yards a day for the first week, then half a mile a day for the second week, and then a mile a day for the third week. Using graded tasks is helpful to promote physical activity because it reduces physical activity-related fatigue, pain, stress, and negative emotional attitude.

About Graded Tasks

Graded tasks begin as easy-to-perform tasks and become increasingly difficult. This approach helps reduce physical activity-related fatigue, pain, stress, and negative emotional attitudes while building confidence and capability.

Review the examples below. Notice how each negative thought is met with a non-judgmental response. These responses show how to acknowledge thoughts kindly without letting them control your actions.

Example 1

Thought: “I’ll never be able to stick with physical activity.”

Non-judgmental response:
“I notice that I’m telling myself I can’t stick with physical activity. What if I asked, ‘What small step could I try today without worrying about the future?’ This thought doesn’t define my ability. I can let it be here without judgment and see what feels doable right now.”

Example 2

Thought: “People will judge me if they see me doing physical activity.”

Non-judgmental response:
“I notice the worry about others’ judgments. What would it be like to turn my attention inward and ask, ‘How does my body feel when I move?’ This feeling of fear doesn’t have to control my choices. I can acknowledge it kindly and let it come and go.”

Now it’s your turn. Read each thought carefully and practice writing your own non-judgmental response. Try using the examples above as a guide. Focus on noticing the thought, acknowledging it with kindness, and exploring a more helpful perspective.
